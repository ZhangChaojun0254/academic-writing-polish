# Editing Rules

Use this reference for general academic polishing, AI-artifact removal, grammar correction, precision, conciseness, citation awareness, and terminology consistency.

## Academic editing principles

### Clarity

- Make the subject, action, and object clear.
- Reduce unnecessary nesting and overly long sentence chains.
- Replace vague expressions with specific academic wording.
- Avoid ambiguity in causal, comparative, and conditional statements.

### Logical flow

- Ensure each paragraph has a clear function.
- Strengthen transitions between background, problem, gap, method, result, and conclusion.
- Avoid sudden concept introduction.
- Do not let conclusions exceed the available evidence.

### Precision

- Use domain-standard terms rather than decorative synonyms.
- Distinguish correlation, causation, association, improvement, robustness, effectiveness, and generalization.
- Avoid unsupported intensifiers such as “significant”, “substantial”, “remarkable”, “明显”, “显著”, and “有效” unless evidence is present.

### Conciseness

- Remove redundant modifiers, repeated ideas, and empty claims.
- Prefer direct academic expression over inflated wording.
- Keep necessary technical detail; concision must not reduce accuracy.

### Formality

- Avoid colloquial, promotional, emotional, or absolute language.
- Avoid “perfectly”, “always”, “never”, “completely”, “颠覆性”, “革命性”, unless strictly justified.
- Maintain a formal but readable academic tone.

### Consistency

Check consistency of:

- terms and translations;
- abbreviations and their first definitions;
- variables, notation, and equation references;
- figure, table, section, and algorithm labels;
- tense and voice;
- citation style and reference format.

## AI-artifact removal

### English patterns to avoid

- “In recent years, X has garnered significant attention...”
- “Furthermore”, “Moreover”, “Additionally” used mechanically.
- “It is worth noting that...” when it adds no information.
- “a myriad of”, “a plethora of”, “paradigm shift”, “cutting-edge”.
- “paving the way for future research”.
- “This study provides valuable insights...” without specifying the actual contribution.
- Overly symmetrical paragraph structures.
- Generic contribution claims without evidence.

### Chinese patterns to avoid

- “近年来，X 引起了广泛关注”
- “值得注意的是”
- “不可忽视的是”
- “具有重要的理论意义和实践价值”
- “为……提供了新的思路/视角”
- “进一步推动了……的发展”
- “实验结果表明，该方法具有良好的效果”
- Overuse of “进行”: “进行研究” → “研究”; “进行分析” → “分析”; “进行建模” → “建模”.

### Replacement principle

Do not merely replace stock phrases with synonyms. Rewrite the sentence so that it states a concrete problem, method, result, limitation, or contribution.

## Citation and evidence rules

- Do not invent references, authors, years, journals, DOIs, or citation numbers.
- Strong claims, field-status claims, comparative statements, and non-common-knowledge assertions usually need citations.
- If citations are missing, either soften the claim or mark `[citation needed]` / “此处需补充引用” when notes are allowed.
- Keep claims proportional to the provided evidence.
- Do not strengthen “suggests” into “proves” unless the evidence truly supports it.

## Grammar and syntax

### English

Check articles, prepositions, subject–verb agreement, singular/plural forms, tense, parallel structure, punctuation, and modifier placement.

Use past tense for completed experiments and methods. Use present tense for established knowledge, paper organization, figure descriptions, and general conclusions.

### Chinese

Check sentence completeness, collocation, unclear reference, overlong sentences, repeated subjects, punctuation, and abstract noun stacking.

Avoid administrative or slogan-like expressions in academic prose.
