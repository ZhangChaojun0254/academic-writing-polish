# Section and Style Guidelines

Use this reference for automatic context inference, section-specific editing, target-style adaptation, Chinese thesis polishing, and English academic paper polishing.

## Automatic context inference

When the user does not specify a field, journal, or writing scene, infer it from:

- title, abstract, keywords, terminology, formulas, methods, datasets, and references;
- section headings such as Abstract, Introduction, Related Work, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusion;
- Chinese thesis markers such as “本文”, “本章”, “毕业论文”, “学位论文”;
- submission markers such as journal name, manuscript, response letter, reviewer comments, or cover letter.

Use the inferred context to adjust terminology density, formality, and sentence structure. If inference is uncertain, use general academic style.

## Target-style adaptation

When the user provides a journal, conference, thesis requirement, supervisor preference, or reference sample, adapt to it by considering:

- sentence length and complexity;
- active/passive voice balance;
- terminology preferences;
- paragraph structure and topic sentence placement;
- contribution phrasing;
- citation density and placement;
- figure/table/result description style;
- degree of compression and formality.

Do not mechanically imitate wording from reference samples. Extract style features and apply them naturally.

## Section-specific guidelines

### Abstract

Cover problem, method, main result, and conclusion. Keep it compact. Avoid background overload and vague contribution claims.

### Introduction

Build a clear chain: background → problem → limitation/gap → proposed work → contribution. Avoid generic openings and unsupported field-wide claims.

### Related Work

Do not simply list studies. Organize by themes, methodological families, or unresolved limitations. Make the research gap explicit.

### Methods

Prioritize precision, reproducibility, variable consistency, and clear process description. Avoid vague verbs such as “handle”, “deal with”, and “process” when specific operations are known.

### Results

Report results before interpreting them. Tie claims to figures, tables, metrics, and comparisons. Avoid overstating performance.

### Discussion

Explain what the results imply, why they occur, what limitations remain, and how they relate to prior work. Do not merely repeat Results.

### Conclusion

Summarize the main contribution and evidence. Avoid broad promises, exaggerated impact, and unsupported future claims.

### Figure and table captions

State what is shown, under what conditions, and what variables or metrics are involved. Do not turn ordinary explanatory sentences into captions.

### Response letter

Respond point-by-point, maintain a professional tone, state what was changed, and identify where it was changed.

## Chinese thesis writing

When polishing Chinese undergraduate, master’s, or doctoral thesis text:

- keep “本文”, “本研究”, and “本章” usage consistent;
- improve paragraph coherence and first-sentence guidance;
- avoid excessive short sections; suggest merging or expansion when appropriate;
- distinguish body explanation from figure captions;
- avoid empty claims such as “具有重要理论意义和实践价值” unless made specific;
- reduce “进行” overuse;
- make chapter transitions explicit;
- maintain formal but natural Chinese academic prose.

If the user asks only for language polish, do not add content. If the user asks to meet thesis standards, limited transition sentences or structural suggestions are acceptable.

## English academic writing

When polishing English academic text:

- prefer clear domain-standard wording over rare synonyms;
- avoid overusing “utilize” when “use” is sufficient;
- keep technical terms stable;
- avoid overlong sentences and excessive nominalization;
- preserve hedging where appropriate;
- do not upgrade cautious claims into stronger claims;
- make contribution statements specific and evidence-bound.
